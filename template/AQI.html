WHAT IS AQI

AQI stands for Air Quality Index. It's a numerical scale used to communicate how polluted or clean the air is in a particular area at a given time. The AQI typically ranges from 0 to 500, with lower values indicating better air quality and higher values indicating worse air quality. Different countries may have their own versions of the AQI, but they generally measure common air pollutants like particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), ozone (O3), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), sulfur dioxide (SO2), and carbon monoxide (CO). AQI values are often categorized into ranges such as "good," "moderate," "unhealthy," "very unhealthy," and "hazardous" to help people understand the health implications of the current air quality.

How Does AQI works

1. The Air Quality Index (AQI) is calculated based on the concentrations of specific air pollutants in the atmosphere. Here's a simplified overview of how it works:

2. Monitoring: Air quality monitoring stations measure the concentrations of various pollutants in the air. These pollutants typically include particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), ozone (O3), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), sulfur dioxide (SO2), and carbon monoxide (CO). The frequency and number of monitoring stations can vary depending on the region and its air quality management system.

3. Pollutant Concentrations: The concentrations of these pollutants are then compared to established air quality standards or guidelines set by government agencies or international organizations like the World Health Organization (WHO). These standards define levels that are considered safe for human health over specific time periods (e.g., hourly, daily, or annual averages).

4. Index Calculation: Each pollutant is assigned a sub-index value based on its concentration relative to the air quality standards. These sub-indices are then combined using a specific formula to calculate the overall AQI. The formula varies slightly between different countries or organizations but generally involves converting pollutant concentrations into a standardized scale and then averaging or taking the maximum value among them.

5. Reporting: The AQI is reported as a single number on a scale from 0 to 500 or sometimes higher. This number indicates the overall air quality at a particular location and time. Additionally, AQI values are often accompanied by descriptive categories (e.g., "good," "moderate," "unhealthy") to help people understand the health implications of the current air quality.

6. Health Implications: Each AQI category corresponds to a range of pollutant concentrations and associated health effects. For example, AQI values in the "good" range typically indicate minimal health risks, while values in the "unhealthy" or "hazardous" range suggest significant risks, especially for sensitive populations such as children, the elderly, and individuals with respiratory or cardiovascular conditions.
